### MANDATORY GATE - Documentation Required (Source: 2.47.28 PM)
> "Every submission **must** include two documents alongside the code: a Product Document and a Technical Document. **Submissions without both documents will not be evaluated, regardless of code quality. This is a hard gate, not a suggestion.**"

#### Required Document 1: Product Document
Answers: *what are you building, for whom, and why?*

Should cover:
- The problem you are solving and why it matters
- Who the target user is (buyer, merchant, or both) and what their current experience looks like
- What you decided to build and the core user journey
- Key product decisions you made and the reasoning behind each
- What you chose NOT to build and why (scope decisions)
- Tradeoffs you encountered and how you resolved them

> "This is not a marketing pitch. It is a thinking document. We want to see how you framed the problem, how you scoped it, and how you made decisions under constraint. **A sharp 2-page product doc is better than a 10-page document full of filler.**"

#### Required Document 2: Technical Document
Answers: *how did you build it, and what happens when things go wrong?*

Should cover:
- System architecture: components, data flow, how they connect
- Key implementation decisions and why you made them
- What AI/LLM does in your system vs. what deterministic code handles - and why you drew the line where you did
- Failure handling: what happens when the Shopify API is down, when the LLM returns garbage, when user input is unexpected? **How does your system respond?**
- Known limitations and what you would improve with more time

> "We are not looking for textbook architecture diagrams. We are looking for evidence that you thought about how your system actually works - including when it breaks. **A team that says 'if the LLM returns a malformed response, we retry once and then show a fallback' has thought harder than a team with a perfect-looking diagram and no failure handling.**"

### Additional Builder Signals (recommended, not mandatory) (Source: 2.47.28 PM)
> Beyond the two required documents, the following will strengthen your submission. These are not mandatory, but strong submissions almost always include them:
- **Decision Log** - a running list of decisions made during the build: "We considered X, chose Y, because Z." Can be a simple markdown file in your repo. The single strongest signal that a team thinks before it codes. **Hardest thing to fake.**
- **Git History** - your commit history tells a story. Atomic commits with meaningful messages ("add checkout recovery flow for shipping confusion") show engineering discipline. A single "final submission" commit shows the opposite. We will look at your git history as a signal of how you work, not just what you build.

> "Why documentation matters this much: At Kasparro, every feature starts with a product requirement document. Engineers produce architecture docs and technical designs before writing code. Documentation is not an afterthought - it is how we build. If you join the team, this is the working style from day one. **This hackathon filters for people who already work this way.**"

## 6. Challenge Tracks (5 total, choose ONE)

**Source: 2.47.42 PM, 2.47.45 PM, 2.47.48 PM**

> "Choose one of the following five problem statements. Each is a real product problem in agentic commerce. Your job is to build a working solution that is focused, useful, and clearly thought through."

### Track 1: AI Shopping Agent (Source: 2.47.42 PM / 2.47.45 PM)
Build an AI shopping agent that helps users discover the right products across Shopify merchants and move from intent to purchase.

> "Think of this as replacing the tedious browse-search-filter-compare cycle with a single conversation. The agent should understand what the user actually needs, narrow options intelligently, compare products on dimensions that matter, explain its recommendations clearly, and support a clean path toward cart or checkout."

What a strong submission looks like:
- Understands user intent deeply - asks smart follow-up questions instead of showing everything
- Narrows options with clear reasoning, not just keyword matching
- Handles tradeoffs explicitly (price vs. quality, availability vs. preference)
- Explains why it recommends what it recommends
- Supports a clean path toward purchase - not just a product list

### Track 2: AI-Assisted Checkout Recovery (Source: 2.47.45 PM)
Build an AI-assisted checkout experience that identifies friction, recovers incomplete sessions, and helps the buyer complete the purchase.

> "Cart abandonment is the biggest revenue leak in e-commerce. Most recovery today is a follow-up email sent hours later. Build something that intervenes in real time - understanding what is blocking the buyer and resolving it before they leave."

What a strong submission looks like:
- Detects what is blocking completion (price hesitation, shipping confusion, trust gap, missing info)
- Asks only the necessary follow-up questions - not a survey, a recovery conversation
- Handles common friction points: coupon confusion, size uncertainty, delivery timelines, payment options
- Reduces drop-off measurably compared to a standard checkout

### Track 3: AI Checkout Copilot (Source: 2.47.48 PM)
Build a buying experience that uses AI to reduce friction during the entire checkout flow - from cart to confirmation.

> "This is different from Track 2. Track 2 recovers broken checkouts. Track 3 prevents friction from happening in the first place. Think of this as an AI layer that sits alongside checkout and proactively explains fees, delivery options, bundle savings, and payment tradeoffs so the buyer never gets confused."

What a strong submission looks like:
- Maintains context from discovery through to purchase - no repetitive questions
- Explains delivery timelines, fees, bundles, and tradeoffs clearly and proactively
- Reduces unnecessary steps - the AI removes confusion, not adds interface
- Creates a polished, trustworthy buying experience end-to-end

### Track 4: AI Customer Support Agent for Commerce (Source: 2.47.48 PM)
Build a store-native AI agent for a Shopify merchant that handles the full spectrum of customer support: pre-purchase questions, policy explanations, order tracking, and returns/refunds.

> "Most support bots are FAQ search wrappers. Build one that **actually understands** the store - its products, its policies, its edge cases - and handles real customer conversations end to end without escalating everything to a human."

What a strong submission looks like:
- Answers product questions grounded in actual store data, **not hallucinated specs**
- Explains policies (returns, shipping, warranty) clearly and accurately
- Handles order status, tracking, and return initiation as real workflows, not just text responses
- Knows when to escalate to a human and does so gracefully
- Avoids overclaiming - handles uncertainty responsibly

### Track 5 (Advanced): AI Representation Optimizer (Source: 2.47.48 PM)
This track is optional and is the most product-intensive challenge. It is closest to the problems Kasparro works on every day. Students who build something strong here will get the most direct signal into Kasparro's internship evaluation.

Build a merchant-facing tool that helps Shopify stores understand how AI shopping agents perceive and represent them - and what they should improve.

> "When an AI agent recommends products, it pulls from store data: product descriptions, images, policies, reviews, structured data. If that data is incomplete, ambiguous, or contradictory, the AI will either skip the merchant or misrepresent them. **This track asks you to build the diagnostic layer that makes that problem visible and actionable.**"

What a strong submission looks like:
- Identifies gaps in a merchant's AI readiness: missing FAQ coverage, ambiguous policies, weak trust signals, unclear product information
- Prioritizes improvements - not just a list of problems, but a ranked action plan
- Shows how AI agents currently perceive the store vs. how the merchant wants to be represented
- Helps merchants take concrete action to improve their AI representation quality
- Demonstrates genuine product thinking - not just a technical scan, but insight into what matters for conversion

## 16. Strategic Implications for Winning

### A. Pick the right track
- **Track 5 (AI Representation Optimizer) is the highest-leverage track for the internship goal.** It is explicitly the closest to Kasparro's core product, and the rules state it gives "the most direct signal into Kasparro's internship evaluation." The career prize (₹20K+₹5K/mo for 6 months + ESOPs path) is **dramatically larger** than the ₹15K 1st prize, so optimizing for the internship evaluation > optimizing for raw 1st-place rubric points.
- Track 5 is also **the least crowded** because it is labeled "Advanced" and product-intensive - many teams will avoid it. Lower competition + higher internship signal = highest expected value.
- **Track 2 (Checkout Recovery) and Track 3 (Checkout Copilot) are differentiated by timing** - Track 2 = recovery after friction, Track 3 = prevention before friction. Be very careful to scope which one you pick; submitting Track 3 work labeled as Track 2 is a positioning failure.
- Track 1 (Shopping Agent) is the most obvious / will likely be the most crowded. To win Track 1 you need to be sharper than ~50% of submissions.

### B. The mandatory documentation gate is the easiest filter to fail
- "Submissions without both documents will not be evaluated, regardless of code quality. **This is a hard gate, not a suggestion.**"
- Many strong technical teams will skip or rush this. **Writing the Product Doc and Technical Doc first** (before code) is a free competitive moat.
- The Decision Log is recommended but is "the single strongest signal that a team thinks before it codes - hardest thing to fake." Add it. Cost: trivial. Benefit: large.

### C. Judging weights tell you exactly where to invest
- **50% combined weight on Product Thinking + Technical Execution = the docs and architecture matter as much as the build.** Most hackathon teams underweight docs. Don't.
- Failure handling is explicitly called out as a tiebreaker: "A team that says 'if the LLM returns a malformed response, we retry once and then show a fallback' has thought harder than a team with a perfect-looking diagram and no failure handling." Document at least 3 failure modes and how you handle them.
- Originality & Insight is only 15%, but distinguishes top 5 from middle of the pack. Pick a non-obvious angle.

### D. Git history is a graded artifact
- "We will look at your git history as a signal of how you work, not just what you build." Do **not** push one mega-commit at the end. Commit atomically with descriptive messages from day 1.

### E. Team composition advantages
- Cross-discipline teams (MBA/arts + technical builder) are explicitly encouraged. If you can pair with a strong product thinker, do so.
- Solo is allowed but you face the same quality bar - multiplies workload risk against the 11-day window.

### F. Hidden requirements easy to miss
- **One submission per team or individual; you cannot submit to multiple tracks.** Pick one track and commit.
- **Each student in only one team.** No double-dipping.
- **3rd/4th year UG, or 1st/2nd year PG** - earlier-year students are not eligible.
- Demo video must be **3-5 minutes** with **narration** (not silent screen recording).
- README must include the demo video link.
- Contribution note is mandatory even for solo participants (must explain how you split product vs. engineering time).
- Late submissions = hard reject. 11:59 PM IST on 20th May 2026 is the wall.

### G. Documentation is a product-thinking artifact, not a marketing pitch
- "A sharp 2-page product doc is better than a 10-page document full of filler." Ruthlessly trim.
- Include scope decisions: **what you decided NOT to build, and why**. This is explicitly called out as a desired signal.
- The product doc should answer: problem, target user, core user journey, key decisions + reasoning, scope cuts, tradeoffs.
